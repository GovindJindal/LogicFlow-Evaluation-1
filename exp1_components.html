Knowledge — Resistor
What is a Resistor?

A passive two-terminal component that opposes current flow. The ratio of voltage to current defines its resistance. Used for current limiting, voltage division, and biasing active components.

V = I × R   |   P = I² × R
Reading 5-Band Color Codes
  • Bands 1–3 are digits, Band 4 is multiplier, Band 5 is tolerance
  • Read left to right; gold/silver band is always on the right
  • Tolerance shows how much the actual value may deviate
  • Power rating (¼W, ½W, 1W) limits maximum safe heat dissipation
  • Series: R_total = R₁ + R₂   Parallel: 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂

Knowledge — Capacitor
What is a Capacitor?

A passive component that stores electrical energy in an electric field between two conductive plates separated by a dielectric. Charges when connected to voltage, discharges when disconnected.

Q = C × V   |   E = ½ C V²
Key Properties
  • Measured in Farads — typical: µF (10⁻⁶), nF (10⁻⁹), pF (10⁻¹²)
  • Electrolytic: polarized, stripe = negative side. Larger values, lower frequency
  • Ceramic/Film: non-polarized, excellent for high-frequency decoupling
  • Blocks DC at steady state, passes AC signals freely
  • Time constant: τ = RC determines charge/discharge speed
  • Exceeding rated voltage causes dielectric breakdown — explosion risk!
⚠ Always verify polarity before connecting electrolytic capacitors. Reverse polarity causes dangerous pressure build-up.

Knowledge — Inductor
What is an Inductor?

A passive component that stores energy in a magnetic field when current flows through it. Resists changes in current (Lenz's Law). Consists of a coil of wire, often wound around a magnetic core to increase inductance.

V = L × (dI/dt)   |   XL = 2πfL
Key Properties
  • Measured in Henrys (H); practical: mH (10⁻³), µH (10⁻⁶)
  • Short circuit to DC steady-state; blocks high-frequency AC
  • Reactance (XL) increases with frequency — opposite of capacitor
  • Iron/ferrite core multiplies inductance by µᵣ (permeability)
  • Used in: power supply filters, RF chokes, LC oscillators, transformers
  • Mutual inductance between two coils is the principle of transformers

Knowledge — PN Junction Diode
What is a Diode?

A semiconductor device with a P-N junction that allows current to flow in one direction only. When forward biased (anode + > cathode −), current flows; when reverse biased, only negligible leakage current passes.

I = Is(e^(V/VT) − 1)   [Shockley Equation]
Types of Diodes
  • Rectifier Diode: Converts AC to DC — core of power supplies
  • Zener Diode: Voltage regulation via controlled reverse breakdown
  • Schottky Diode: Very fast switching, low forward voltage (~0.2V)
  • LED: Emits light when forward biased (see LED tab)
  • Photodiode: Generates current proportional to incident light
  • Varactor: Variable capacitance diode used in RF tuning circuits

Knowledge — LED
How does an LED work?

A Light Emitting Diode converts electrical energy directly into light via electroluminescence. When electrons recombine with holes across the P-N junction, they release energy as photons. The photon color depends on the semiconductor bandgap energy.

E = hf = hc/λ    (Photon Energy)
Circuit Rules
  • Longer lead = Anode (+), Shorter lead = Cathode (−)
  • Always use current-limiting resistor: R = (Vs − Vf) / If
  • Typical current: 10–20 mA for standard indicator LEDs
  • Color determines forward voltage drop (see material table below)
  • White LEDs use a blue InGaN chip + yellow YAG phosphor coating
💡 Active LED: AlGaAs, GaAsP — Red, 610–760 nm

Knowledge — Digital Multimeter
What is a Multimeter?

A multi-function electronic test instrument combining voltmeter, ammeter, and ohmmeter in a single device. Modern digital multimeters (DMM) display readings on an LCD and include auto-ranging for the correct measurement scale.

Terminal Connections
  • COM: Always connect black probe here (common ground reference)
  • V/Ω/Hz: Red probe for voltage, resistance, frequency, diode, capacitance
  • mA/A: Red probe ONLY when measuring current — never for voltage!
How to Measure
  • Voltage: Connect in PARALLEL across the component being tested
  • Current: Connect in SERIES — you must break the circuit
  • Resistance: Measure with component de-energized (power OFF)
  • Diode test: Forward shows ~0.6–0.7V; reverse shows "OL"
  • Continuity: Beeps when resistance is below ~50Ω
⚠ Never measure resistance/continuity on a live (powered) circuit — this will damage the meter!
⚠ Never put red probe in mA/A jack then measure voltage — internal fuse will blow!

Knowledge — Breadboard
What is a Breadboard?

A solderless prototyping board with spring-clip contacts inside each hole. Component leads are inserted to make electrical contact without soldering — perfect for testing circuits before committing to a PCB design.

Internal Connection Layout
  • Power rails (top & bottom) run horizontally the full board length
  • Component rows run vertically — 5 holes per group (A–E or F–J)
  • The center gap divides rows — IC chips straddle it for dual access
  • Maximum current per rail: typically 1–3A depending on board quality
Pro Tips
  • Color-code wires: Red = VCC, Black = GND, others = signals
  • Keep wires short and close to the board surface for clarity
  • Place ICs straddling the center gap for full pin access
  • Add 100nF bypass capacitors near IC power pins to reduce noise
  • Use continuity mode on multimeter to verify connections
